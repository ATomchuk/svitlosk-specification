# Alternative Models

## Model A: Five Primitives (META-007A)

**Primitives:** Attention, Observation, Comparison, Judgment, Recognition

**Strengths:**

- Covers basic cognitive operations
- Each primitive is distinct
- Dependency graph is clear

**Weaknesses:**

- Circular dependencies between Attention, Observation, Comparison
- Judgment and Recognition may not be truly primitive
- Missing SELECTING, INTERPRETING, DIFFERENTIATING

---

## Model B: Four Primitives

**Primitives:** Attention, Observation, Judgment, Recognition

**Strengths:**

- Removes Comparison (which has circular dependency)
- More minimal than Model A
- Judgment and Recognition are the most independent

**Weaknesses:**

- Cannot explain how Comparison works
- May miss essential relating operation

---

## Model C: Network Without Primitives

**Definition:** No primitives exist — all operations are interconnected in a network.

**Strengths:**

- Avoids the problem of defining "primitive"
- Acknowledges circular dependencies
- More realistic model of cognition

**Weaknesses:**

- Cannot identify irreducible elements
- May be too complex
- Does not provide minimal kernel

---

## Model D: Three Primitives

**Primitives:** Attention, Judgment, Recognition

**Strengths:**

- Most minimal set
- Attention is root
- Judgment and Recognition are most independent

**Weaknesses:**

- Cannot explain Observation
- Cannot explain Comparison
- May be too minimal

---

## Model E: Six Primitives

**Primitives:** Attention, Observation, Comparison, Judgment, Recognition, Memory

**Strengths:**

- Adds Memory as essential primitive
- Acknowledges that thinking requires persistence

**Weaknesses:**

- Memory may be composite (storage + retrieval)
- May be too large

# Key Insight

**No single model is clearly superior.**

**Model A (META-007A) is ONE valid model, but not the ONLY valid model.**

**The choice of primitives depends on what we consider "fundamental."**
